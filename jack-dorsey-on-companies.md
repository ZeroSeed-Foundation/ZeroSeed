# Key Insights, Learnings, and Practical Tips

## Core Thesis

Jack Dorsey’s argument is that AI is not just a productivity tool. It may force companies to rethink their entire structure.

Traditional companies are built around hierarchy because humans needed managers to move information up, down, and across the organization. But if every Slack message, meeting, doc, pull request, customer interaction, and decision creates a digital artifact, then AI can sit on top of those artifacts and make the company more legible.

The company becomes less like a pyramid and more like an intelligence system.

## The Problem With Traditional Hierarchy

Traditional hierarchy exists mainly to manage information flow.

Managers collect, summarize, filter, and relay information. That creates delays, distortion, politics, and information loss.

In an AI-native company, the goal is to reduce dependence on human chains of communication and let anyone query the company directly.

Practical takeaway:

- Ask: “What information currently travels through managers that could instead be made directly accessible?”
- Record and structure more company work so it becomes queryable.
- Treat internal artifacts as strategic data, not exhaust.

## The Company as an Intelligence

Dorsey describes a company as a kind of intelligence already: people, tools, data, customers, decisions, and feedback loops.

AI makes it possible to model that intelligence.

A future company could have:

- A company world model
- A customer world model
- Real-time querying of company knowledge
- Proactive recommendations
- Less reliance on static roadmaps
- More direct customer-driven product development

Practical takeaway:

Build a system where employees can ask:

- What are customers asking for most?
- Where are we blocked?
- What are we building that is not aligned with strategy?
- What decisions were made and why?
- What customer problems are emerging?

## The New Org Model

Dorsey wants to simplify Block toward three core roles:

### 1. ICs: Builders and Operators

These are the people doing the work: engineers, designers, salespeople, product people, operators.

Their durable human skills are:

- Judgment
- Taste
- Creativity
- Craft

### 2. DRIs: Owners of Outcomes

DRIs own customer outcomes, strategy, roadmap direction, and accountability.

Their durable human skills are:

- Ownership
- Accountability
- Strategic judgment

### 3. Player Coaches

These replace many traditional managers. Their job is to build human capability by doing the work and coaching others.

Their durable human skills are:

- Coaching
- Empathy
- Craft development
- Raising standards

Practical takeaway:

Audit every manager role and ask:

- Are they building?
- Are they owning outcomes?
- Are they coaching?
- Or are they mainly routing information?

## Flattening the Company

Block’s current max depth is about five layers from CEO to any employee. Dorsey wants that down to two or three.

The extreme version: everyone reports to the CEO, with AI handling much of the coordination layer.

Practical takeaway:

Measure organizational depth.

Ask:

- How many layers exist between CEO and customer-facing work?
- Which layers exist only to summarize or coordinate?
- Which layers actually improve judgment, execution, or coaching?

## AI Should Not Make All Decisions

Both Dorsey and Roelof Botha push back on the idea that AI should make most decisions.

AI should improve the context for human decisions.

The future decision system is:

- Customers provide signal
- AI organizes and interprets signal
- Humans apply judgment, taste, values, and strategy

Practical takeaway:

Use AI as better input, not final output.

Do not blindly delegate judgment to AI.

## Customers Become the Roadmap

Dorsey argues that the roadmap is a company’s limiting factor.

In the future, customers may expect to ask for features and get them composed in real time.

The company’s job becomes building:

1. Capabilities  
2. Interfaces  
3. Proactive intelligence  
4. World models  

If customers repeatedly ask for something the system cannot deliver, that gap becomes the roadmap.

Practical takeaway:

Start tracking customer requests conversationally and structurally.

Ask:

- What are customers trying to do that our product does not support?
- What do they repeatedly hack around?
- What requests reveal missing capabilities?

## Distribution Becomes More Important

AI makes building easier, which means more products, more noise, and more competition.

As creation gets cheaper, distribution and attention become more valuable.

Practical takeaway:

Do not only ask, “Can we build this?”

Also ask:

- How will anyone discover it?
- Why will customers trust us?
- What distribution advantage do we have?
- What signal do we own that others do not?

## AI-Native Transformation Is Structural, Not Incremental

Many companies think AI means every employee becomes 10x more productive.

Dorsey says that is too small.

The real opportunity is to redesign how the company works.

Practical takeaway:

Do not just add copilots.

Ask:

- If we started the company today with AI, would we structure it this way?
- Would we have the same number of people?
- Would we have the same layers?
- Would we plan the same way?
- Would we manage the same way?

## How Meetings Change

Old meeting: slide deck or document.

New meeting: prototype.

At Block, people increasingly bring working prototypes instead of presentations.

This allows faster debate, clearer judgment, and real-time iteration.

Practical takeaway:

Replace status decks with demos.

Meeting rule:

> Bring the artifact, prototype, customer signal, or working model — not just a narrative.

## Focus vs Exploration

Historically, focus was critical because exploring multiple paths was expensive.

AI lowers the cost of exploration.

Companies can now explore more options in parallel, then focus deeply once the right path emerges.

Practical takeaway:

Use AI for breadth, humans for depth.

- Explore many directions quickly
- Prototype cheaply
- Use human judgment to pick
- Spend craft and taste on the final 20%

## The Last 20% Matters Most

Dorsey says AI can often get you 80% of the way.

The last 20% depends on:

- Taste
- Judgment
- Creativity
- Detail
- Craft
- Point of view

Practical takeaway:

Do not mistake “working” for “excellent.”

AI raises the floor. Humans still raise the ceiling.

## Layoffs and Rebuilding

Dorsey describes Block’s major restructuring as a proactive move, not a reaction.

They asked:

1. What is the minimum team needed to keep services running?
2. What is the minimum team needed for compliance and legal obligations?
3. What is the minimum team needed to grow and rebuild the company as an intelligence?

Practical takeaway:

When redesigning an org, start from first principles rather than trimming around the edges.

Ask:

- What must exist?
- What creates customer value?
- What protects the company?
- What helps us grow?
- What exists only because of old structure?

## Board Building Advice

Dorsey: your first board is your investors. Treat investor selection like hiring someone you can never fire — who can fire you.

Roelof Botha: choosing an investor board member is a recruiting decision, not just a financing decision.

Practical tips:

- Choose the person, not the firm brand.
- Add a strong independent board member early.
- Do not wait until IPO preparation to build the board.
- Build trust before crisis.
- Choose board members who can handle bold, uncomfortable ideas.

## CEO Lessons

Dorsey defines the CEO role historically as:

1. Set principles, values, team, and culture
2. Ensure decisions are made in customer and market context
3. Raise the execution bar

In the AI-native future, the CEO also becomes an architect of the company intelligence.

Practical takeaway:

The CEO’s job shifts from managing the pyramid to aligning the system.

## Delegation Can Go Too Far

Dorsey says one of his biggest regrets was delegating too much.

At Block, separate business units started to feel like a holding company rather than one integrated company.

Practical takeaway:

Delegate execution, but do not abdicate learning, architecture, or coherence.

Ask:

- Are teams optimizing locally or company-wide?
- Are we one company or several loosely connected businesses?
- What am I avoiding learning by delegating?

## Second Acts Require Losing Credibility

Cash App was disliked internally for years. Investors did not understand it. The board questioned it.

Dorsey says leaders must sometimes be willing to lose credibility temporarily to pursue something important.

Practical takeaway:

For bold bets, be clear on:

- Why this matters
- What principle supports it
- How long you are willing to defend it
- What evidence would prove or disprove it
- How you will earn credibility back

## Learning as a CEO Skill

Dorsey’s biggest regrets come from moments when he chose not to learn.

He reframed every person, problem, conflict, and criticism as a mentor.

Practical takeaway:

After major events, ask:

- What is this trying to teach me?
- What am I ignoring?
- Where am I being stubborn?
- What mistake might I repeat if I do not learn now?

## Meditation and CEO Performance

Dorsey frames meditation not as mystical, but as physical training for the mind.

Benefits:

- Sharper focus
- Better observation
- Less emotional reactivity
- More deliberate action
- More comfort with discomfort

Practical takeaway:

The CEO value is not “being calm.” It is creating space between stimulus and response.

## Timeless CEO Qualities

Roelof Botha’s acronym: ALE

### Authenticity

People see who you really are.

### Logic

You are rational, predictable, and clear.

### Empathy

You care about people, customers, and the business.

## New CEO Qualities for the AI Era

Dorsey emphasizes the ability to reprogram your own mind.

Modern CEOs need to:

- Question assumptions constantly
- Challenge sacred cows
- Move fast
- Discern signal from noise
- Use AI as input
- Maintain a strong point of view
- Preserve taste and judgment
- Avoid copying what everyone else is doing

## The Danger of Copycat Companies

AI makes it easier to build, so many companies will become copies of copies.

Differentiation comes from point of view.

Practical takeaway:

Ask:

- What do we believe that others do not?
- What are we building because we want it to exist?
- What is opinionated here?
- Where are we pushing boundaries?
- What would be uncomfortable but right?

## Practical Playbook for a 100-Person Company

### Step 1: Make the company legible

Capture and organize:

- Customer conversations
- Sales calls
- Support tickets
- Product decisions
- Roadmap debates
- Code changes
- Meeting notes
- Strategy docs

### Step 2: Put intelligence on top

Create a system employees can query.

Example questions:

- What are the biggest customer pain points?
- Which projects are stalled?
- What decisions contradict our strategy?
- What do customers keep asking for?
- Where are we duplicating work?

### Step 3: Reduce hierarchy

Identify layers that mostly pass information around.

Replace some management work with:

- Better systems
- Clearer ownership
- Direct access to information
- Coaching assignments

### Step 4: Normalize roles

Move toward:

- Builders/operators
- Outcome owners
- Player coaches

### Step 5: Replace decks with prototypes

Require demos, working models, customer evidence, or artifacts.

### Step 6: Use AI for exploration

Prototype more paths faster.

Then use human taste and judgment to choose.

### Step 7: Rebuild around customer signal

Let customer behavior and requests expose capability gaps.

Use those gaps to shape the roadmap.

### Step 8: Preserve human judgment

Do not let AI become the decider.

Let it make the company more context-rich, transparent, and responsive.

> The roadmap may be the limiting factor.

> Customers should help build alongside the company.

> Use AI as input, not output.

> The last 20% is taste, judgment, and creativity.

> Every leader has to be comfortable losing credibility for a while.

## Bottom Line

The big idea is that AI-native companies may not look like today’s companies with AI tools added.

They may have fewer layers, fewer traditional managers, more builders, more direct customer signal, more prototypes, more transparency, and more reliance on human judgment at the edges of an intelligent system.

The durable advantage will not be “using AI.”

It will be redesigning the company around intelligence, customer signal, speed, taste, and judgment.
